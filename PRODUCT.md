# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

**Primary**: Homeowner planning a renovation — they have time to compare options, they read the details, and they want to feel genuinely confident before they pick up the phone. They arrive with some skepticism (too many bad contractor experiences) and leave only when they believe this company is different. They're often browsing on a laptop in the evening, cross-referencing a few sites before deciding who to call.

**Secondary**: Existing customer returning to book a follow-up job or refer someone — they already trust the brand, they just need the fastest path to contact.

**Context**: New York area. The business serves residential plumbing, handyman, and light renovation work. Content is placeholder (PLUMBER., `(001) 123 456 789`, `5241 Elgin St. Celina, 10258`) until real details are supplied by the client.

## Product Purpose

A single-page marketing site that converts renovation-planning homeowners into booked appointments. It exists to answer one question before it's asked: *"Can I trust these people with my home?"* Success looks like a visitor reading through at least two sections and clicking the contact CTA — not just landing and bouncing.

## Brand Personality

**Trustworthy. Capable. Unhurried.**

Voice is direct and warm, never salesy. Copy should read like a confident tradesperson talking to a homeowner — clear, specific, zero filler. Emotional goal: the visitor should feel *relieved*, not pressured. Like finding a mechanic you actually trust.

## Anti-references

- **Generic local trades directory**: yellow-pages energy, stock-photo overload, price-comparison table layouts, "CALL NOW" banners in 48px red bold. The design should feel nothing like Yelp or Angi.
- **Dated small-business builder site**: Wix/Squarespace cookie-cutter layouts, clipart wrench icons, tiled pattern backgrounds, aggressive promo banners ("50% OFF TODAY ONLY!"), default CMS font stacks. This site should not look like it was assembled from a template in an afternoon.

## Design Principles

1. **Proof over promise.** Every section that makes a claim should show evidence — project photos, real stats, named testimonials, credentials. A renovation planner is a comparison shopper; assertions without backing read as noise.

2. **Confidence at first glance.** Hierarchy should communicate authority before a single word is processed. Layout, spacing, and typographic weight do the trust-building; the copy confirms it. Never let a section feel tentative or visually under-resolved.

3. **Trade-grade polish.** Premium and precise without feeling like a tech startup or an overdesigned portfolio. The aesthetic should match the craft: clean joints, no rough edges, nothing showy for its own sake.

4. **Calm converts.** No countdown timers, no pop-up overlays, no aggressive CTA repetition. A homeowner who feels pressured clicks away. The path to booking should feel like a natural next step — obvious, not insisted upon.

5. **Frictionless path to the appointment.** The contact CTA is never more than one scroll away on any section. Conversion is the whole job of this page; every design decision should serve that path without being heavy-handed about it.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA minimum. Specific considerations:
- Color contrast: accent `#2B9FE3` on white passes AA for large text only — verify all body-size text uses ink or muted tones, not accent, for critical content.
- Motion: wrap any scroll-triggered animations in `prefers-reduced-motion` media queries; the primary audience skews older homeowners.
- Touch targets: mobile CTA buttons and nav links must meet 44×44px minimum — renovation planners often browse on tablets.
- Form labels: the appointment form must have visible labels (not placeholder-only), with clear error states.
